How Do We Add Redundancy?

6 min read · Boaz Micah Quantum Error Correction

How do we add redundancy in

quantum error correction?

In the previous article, we identified three obstacles that make quantum

error correction hard: the no-cloning theorem, the continuous nature of

quantum errors, and the destructiveness of measurement. In this article,

we tackle the first of these head-on: how do you add redundancy to

quantum information without being allowed to copy it?

· · ·

The Problem with Copying

In the classical repetition code, redundancy was straightforward:

copy the logical bit three times, and use majority voting to recover

from errors. The quantum analogue of this would be a map of the

form:

The no-cloning theorem tells us this is impossible. No physical

operation can duplicate an unknown quantum state. So the most

direct translation of the classical strategy is ruled out from the start.

But this doesn’t mean redundancy is impossible. It just means we

have to be more careful about what redundancy means in the

quantum setting.

Redundancy Through Entanglement

In quantum information, redundancy is achieved not by copying, but

by expanding the Hilbert space. Instead of duplicating the state, we

entangle the qubit we want to protect with additional qubits,

spreading the logical information across a larger system.

The Codespace

state becomes:

This state is no longer in the codespace C. It has been rotated into a

different two-dimensional subspace:

We call F the error subspace. Crucially, C and F are mutually

orthogonal: they share no overlap. This is the key insight. An

uncorrupted logical state lives in C; a state that has suffered a bit-flip

on the first qubit lives in F; and these two possibilities are, in

principle, perfectly distinguishable.

And here is where the geometry pays off. Because C and F are

orthogonal, we can perform a projective measurement that

determines which subspace the state occupies, without learning

anything about the values of α and β.

Think of the quantum state as a vector living in an eight-dimensional

space, spanned by the eight computational basis states: {|000⟩, |001⟩,

|010⟩, |011⟩, |100⟩, |101⟩, |110⟩, |111⟩}. When no error has occurred,

the state lives entirely within the codespace. That is, it has

components only along the |000⟩ and |111⟩ axes. Projecting onto any

other axis gives zero.

When an error occurs, the state is rotated out of the codespace. It

now has a non-zero component along one of the other axes, and

which axis that is tells us exactly what error occurred. We can detect

this by performing a projective measurement that asks a simple

question: which subspace does the state live in?

Notice that this measurement never reveals the values of α and β. It

only tells us which error subspace the state has been rotated into.

The actual quantum data we care about remains untouched

throughout. As such, we can detect the error without ever touching

the logical information itself.
